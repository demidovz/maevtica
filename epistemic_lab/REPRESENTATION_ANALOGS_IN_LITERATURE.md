# Mathematical Analogs of the Current Representations

Status: literature comparison for the Representation Completeness Program.

The goal is not to claim novelty prematurely. The goal is to identify the
closest existing mathematical analogs of:

```text
R, K, P, D, S, B, G, M
```

and separate:

1. what is already known;
2. what differs in the Epistemic Lab formulation;
3. which claims in the Representation Completeness Program do not follow
   directly from standard results;
4. where genuine novelty may remain.

No code. No algorithms. No new objectives.

## 1. R: Recurrence / Presentation Relation

### Closest Analogs

1. Hypergraphs and relational structures.
2. Event structures in concurrency.
3. Trace/event logs in process theory.
4. Formal contexts before closure in Formal Concept Analysis.

### What Coincides

`R subset Fin(U)` is mathematically close to a hypergraph: a collection of
finite subsets presented together.

It is also close to the configuration data in event structures, where event
occurrences are constrained by consistency, conflict or enabling relations.

### What Differs

In the Epistemic Lab formulation, `R` is deliberately pre-admissible:

```text
R(X) = X is presented / recurrent / co-available
```

not:

```text
X is valid
X is possible
X is consistent
X is causally enabled
```

Event structures normally already include causality, consistency or conflict.
The Genesis of Admissibility program forbids those at the start.

### Known Theorems

For hypergraphs and set systems, one can study incidence, transversals,
closures, clique complexes and generated complexes.

For event structures, known results connect events, consistency/enabling,
configurations and domain-theoretic models of computation.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
R alone cannot determine a unique K
```

is not a standard hypergraph theorem in this epistemic form. It follows from
the underdetermination of closure: many closures can extend the same presented
hyperedges.

The distinction between:

```text
presentation
```

and:

```text
admissibility
```

is also not the standard starting point in event structures, where consistency
is usually built in.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is treating recurrence as a pre-boundary presentation layer and
asking which non-injective admissibility boundaries can be generated from it
under invariance and conservative closure.

## 2. K: Admissible Configuration Set

### Closest Analogs

1. Abstract simplicial complexes.
2. Independence systems and matroids.
3. Constraint satisfaction relations.
4. Event-structure consistency predicates.
5. Hypergraph families.

### What Coincides

`K subset Fin(U)` is a family of finite subsets. If `K` is downward closed, it
is an abstract simplicial complex or independence system.

If `K` additionally satisfies exchange axioms, it approaches matroid
independence.

If `K` is interpreted as satisfying assignments or allowed local configurations,
it resembles a constraint satisfaction relation.

### What Differs

The Epistemic Lab `K` is not assumed to be:

1. downward closed;
2. exchange-satisfying;
3. generated by constraints over variables;
4. temporal;
5. causal;
6. probabilistic.

This is important. Many known theorems require closure or exchange properties
that `K` does not have by default.

### Known Theorems

For abstract simplicial complexes:

1. faces, facets, skeletons and links;
2. geometric realization;
3. algebraic invariants such as Stanley-Reisner rings.

For matroids:

1. equivalent axiom systems;
2. rank, closure, circuits and flats;
3. greedy optimization under matroid conditions.

For CSP-style relations:

1. consistency notions;
2. polymorphisms and tractability theory;
3. local-to-global questions.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The Representation Completeness claim:

```text
K -> P -> S/D
```

does not require simplicial closure or matroid exchange. It is more general
than those theories.

The claim:

```text
K is complete for admissibility but incomplete for genesis, transfer and
fixed-point theorems
```

is not a standard theorem of simplicial complexes or matroids. It is a
representation-completeness statement about what data `K` does and does not
contain.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is using arbitrary finite admissibility boundaries as an epistemic
representation, then classifying exactly which theorem classes become provable
without adding closure, exchange, topology, probability or dynamics.

## 3. P: Compatibility Profile Family

### Closest Analogs

1. Formal Concept Analysis.
2. Chu spaces.
3. Neighborhood systems in modal/topological semantics.
4. Type/context semantics.
5. FCA-style derivation and Galois connections.

### What Coincides

`P(x) = {C : C union {x} in K}` resembles an object-attribute incidence profile:
each item is classified by the contexts in which it can appear.

Formal Concept Analysis starts from objects, attributes and an incidence
relation, then derives concepts and concept lattices through derivation
operators.

Chu spaces similarly represent information by two domains and a satisfaction or
incidence relation.

### What Differs

In FCA, contexts are usually attributes. In the Epistemic Lab representation,
contexts are themselves finite configurations into which an item may be
inserted.

This makes `P` an insertion-profile representation, not merely an
object-attribute table.

Also, not every arbitrary profile family is accepted. It must satisfy
decomposition coherence to reconstruct a `K`:

```text
X - {x} in P(x) iff X - {y} in P(y)
```

for all `x,y in X`.

### Known Theorems

FCA provides:

1. derivation operators;
2. Galois connections;
3. concept lattices;
4. implication theory;
5. representation of complete lattices as concept lattices.

Chu spaces provide a general relational model of information structure with
dualities and transformations.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The profile-coherence condition for reconstructing finite configuration
admissibility is not a standard FCA theorem in this form.

The representation-completeness claim:

```text
P is proof-efficient for substitutability but not sufficient for transfer or
fixed points without transformations/stages
```

is also outside ordinary FCA.

### Potential Novelty

The likely novelty is the insertion-context profile view:

```text
item meaning = admissible contexts of insertion
```

and its use as a bridge between admissibility, substitutability, distinction
and quotient formation.

## 4. D: Distinction Relation

### Closest Analogs

1. Indiscernibility relations in rough set theory.
2. Apartness / distinguishability relations.
3. Behavioral distinguishability and bisimulation.
4. Equivalence relations and partitions.

### What Coincides

Rough set theory starts with indiscernibility and studies approximation of sets
through equivalence classes.

Bisimulation theory studies when states or systems cannot be distinguished by
allowed observations or moves.

The Epistemic Lab distinction relation:

```text
D_K(a,b) iff P_K(a) != P_K(b)
```

fits the broad pattern: two items are distinguished when their observable or
structural profiles differ.

### What Differs

In rough sets and bisimulation, the distinguishing basis is usually supplied:
features, probes, observations, transitions or modal formulas.

Here, distinction is not a starting relation. It is a projection of
admissibility/profile structure.

Also, `D` is explicitly treated as lossy:

```text
D records that profiles differ, not how they differ.
```

### Known Theorems

Rough sets provide:

1. lower and upper approximations;
2. boundary regions;
3. positive and negative regions;
4. approximation accuracy.

Bisimulation provides:

1. behavioral equivalence;
2. simulation/bisimulation preorders;
3. modal invariance results;
4. partition refinement methods.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
distinction is not equivalent to K because it forgets context profiles
```

is a representation-completeness result. It is not merely the rough-set claim
that indiscernibility classes approximate a target set.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is demoting distinction from primitive epistemic operation to a
lossy projection:

```text
admissibility/profile structure -> distinction
```

and proving that distinction cannot reconstruct the admissibility boundary.

## 5. S: Substitutability Structure

### Closest Analogs

1. Simulation and refinement preorders.
2. Logical implication and entailment preorders.
3. Specialization orders.
4. Abstract interpretation soundness.
5. Galois connections.

### What Coincides

`S(x,y)` as "x can stand for y" is close to refinement or simulation:
one object can replace another without losing specified behavior.

The profile definition:

```text
S(x,y) iff P(y) subset P(x)
```

is an inclusion preorder, analogous to implication, refinement or stronger
specification.

### What Differs

Standard simulation/refinement usually depends on transition systems,
behaviors, observations or specifications.

Epistemic Lab's `S` can be defined statically from admissible insertion
profiles without assuming transitions, tests or behavior.

Also, `S` is intentionally treated as lossy relative to `P`: it records order
but not the contexts that witness the order.

### Known Theorems

Known results include:

1. preorder properties of simulation/refinement;
2. quotienting by mutual simulation;
3. preservation of modal/logical properties under simulation;
4. Galois-connection-based abstraction and soundness.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
S is complete for replacement-order proofs but incomplete for context-sensitive
proofs
```

is not a standard simulation theorem. It is a representation-cost and
translation-power claim.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is isolating substitutability as:

```text
profile inclusion over admissibility
```

and classifying exactly when that preorder is sufficient and when profile data
must be retained.

## 6. B: Boundary Representation

### Closest Analogs

1. Rough set lower/upper/boundary regions.
2. Three-valued logics.
3. Partial functions and partial predicates.
4. Domain-theoretic partial information.
5. Classification boundaries.

### What Coincides

Partial boundary:

```text
(K+, K-, K?)
```

is close to rough set theory's lower approximation, negative region and boundary
region.

Sharp boundary:

```text
B: Fin(U) -> {inside, outside}
```

is simply a characteristic predicate for `K`.

### What Differs

Rough sets normally approximate a target set relative to an indiscernibility
relation. Here, partial `B` represents underdetermination in the genesis of an
admissibility boundary.

So the direction differs:

```text
rough set: indiscernibility -> approximate target
Epistemic Lab: recurrence/invariance -> partial admissibility boundary
```

### Known Theorems

Rough sets provide:

1. lower/upper approximation;
2. boundary region;
3. accuracy measures;
4. rule induction from incomplete data.

Three-valued and partial-predicate traditions provide truth-value and
definedness calculi.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
partial B <-> family of sharp K completions
```

is compatible with rough set thinking, but the use as a genesis-stage object
for admissibility is not standard.

The Representation Completeness claim:

```text
partial B is fundamental for underdetermination, while sharp B is equivalent
to K
```

is a role classification, not a rough-set theorem.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is treating partial boundary as the natural first product of
admissibility genesis, rather than as an approximation of an already-given
target set.

## 7. G: Transformation / Invariance Representation

### Closest Analogs

1. Group actions and invariant theory.
2. Automorphism groups of structures.
3. Equivariant maps.
4. Erlangen-style geometry by transformation groups.
5. Symmetry reduction.

### What Coincides

The representation:

```text
G acts on Fin(U)
```

matches the standard idea that structure should be invariant under a
transformation class.

The orbit idea:

```text
Fin(U) / G
```

is standard: invariant classifications are constant on orbits.

### What Differs

The Epistemic Lab result insists that:

```text
G alone does not recover K.
```

One also needs boundary status per orbit:

```text
Fin(U)/G -> {inside, outside}
```

In much invariant-theory work, the group and the object acted on are already
specified. Here, part of the question is whether `G` is a genesis constraint or
only a later symmetry of `K`.

### Known Theorems

Known results include:

1. orbit-stabilizer style decompositions;
2. invariant functions are constant on orbits;
3. equivariant maps preserve group action structure;
4. automorphism groups preserve definable structure.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
G is fundamental for transfer but incomplete for admissibility
```

is not an invariant-theory theorem. It is a representation-completeness
classification.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is using `G` as the representation responsible for experimental
transfer and feature-ablation invariance, while proving that it cannot replace
admissibility content.

## 8. M: Integrated Meta-Representation

### Closest Analogs

1. Chu spaces.
2. Formal contexts with extra structure.
3. Institutions in model theory / logic.
4. Approximation spaces enriched with transformations.
5. Event structures with observations and consistency.

### What Coincides

The proposed bookkeeping object:

```text
M = (U, Fin(U), R, B_partial, G, Ctx)
```

resembles a structured information space: carrier, configurations,
presentation relation, partial boundary, transformations and context selection.

Chu spaces are especially close at the level of "states/items versus
attributes/contexts with a satisfaction relation".

FCA formal contexts are also close, but usually lack recurrence, partial
boundary and transformation data.

Institutions are analogs if one reads:

```text
models / sentences / satisfaction / change of signature
```

as a multi-representation framework.

### What Differs

`M` is not proposed as a new primitive ontology.

It is explicitly an integration object required because earlier equivalence
tests showed independent data:

1. `R` not recoverable from `K`;
2. `G` not recoverable uniquely from `K`;
3. partial boundary not recoverable from sharp `K`;
4. context restriction not recoverable from `D` or `S`.

### Known Theorems

Chu spaces provide:

1. relational representation of information;
2. duality between states and attributes;
3. transformations preserving satisfaction;
4. categorical structure.

Institutions provide:

1. abstract model theory across logical systems;
2. satisfaction conditions under change of notation/signature.

Event structures provide:

1. configurations;
2. conflict/consistency/enabling;
3. links to domains and process semantics.

### What Does Not Follow From Known Results

The claim:

```text
M is universal as integration, not as reduction
```

is specific to the Representation Completeness Program.

Known frameworks often supply a unifying formalism, but they do not by
themselves prove that the Epistemic Lab representations have exactly the
translation, loss and independence profile documented here.

### Potential Novelty

The novelty is not "inventing a universal mathematical container." Existing
containers exist.

The potential novelty is the representation-completeness criterion:

```text
a meta-object is adequate only if it preserves the independent proof powers of
R, K, P, D, S, B and G rather than collapsing them prematurely.
```

## 10. What Is Already Covered by Existing Mathematics

The following are not novel by themselves:

1. profile-to-lattice constructions in FCA;
2. boundary regions in rough sets;
3. finite set families and their closures;
4. preorders from inclusion;
5. invariants under group actions;
6. simulation/refinement-style replacement;
7. integrated relational information spaces such as Chu spaces.

Any claim of novelty must avoid rebranding these.

## 11. What Remains Potentially New

Potentially new contributions are narrower:

1. The representation-completeness matrix across `R,K,P,D,S,B,G`.
2. The distinction between genesis representations (`R`, partial `B`) and
   admissibility representations (`K`, sharp `B`).
3. The claim that distinction is a lossy projection of admissibility/profile
   structure, not a fundamental process.
4. The profile-coherence condition for reconstructing arbitrary admissible
   finite-configuration boundaries.
5. The classification of predictive power by representation.
6. The thesis that no single representation is globally fundamental, because
   different theorem classes have different minimal representations.
7. The meta-object criterion that integration must preserve independent proof
   powers rather than merely embed everything in a large formalism.
